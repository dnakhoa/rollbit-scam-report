## 1. Executive Summary

This report documents a pattern of systematic fund confiscation, regulatory evasion, and deceptive practices by Rollbit.com, an online cryptocurrency casino, sportsbook, and futures trading platform operated by **Bull Gaming N.V.** (Curacao Company No. 157086).

Across **67 documented victim complaints** spanning from September 2022 to January 2026, users report a combined **$495,210 in confirmed locked or confiscated funds**, with a resolution rate of just **4.5%** (3 out of 67 cases). The true scale of losses is likely significantly larger, as 7 cases had unquantifiable amounts, and many affected users never file public complaints.

The findings presented here are sourced primarily from the BitcoinTalk forum's Scam Accusations section, supplemented by Trustpilot reviews, Casino Guru complaint filings, and cross-referenced against news reporting from CoinDesk, CryptoSlate, BeInCrypto, and CryptoNews.

## 3. Regulatory & Legal Findings

### 3.1 $123 Million in Crypto Assets Seized in Ukraine (May 2025)

On May 9, 2025, the Pechersk District Court of Kyiv authorized the transfer of seized cryptocurrency assets to ARMA (Ukraine's National Agency for Investigation and Asset Management). The seized funds totaled over **$123 million** in BTC, ETH, LTC, SOL, and other cryptocurrencies.

Key facts from the court proceedings:

- Investigators linked the funds to accounts on Binance belonging to a Ukrainian citizen associated with **Bull Gaming N.V.** (Rollbit's operator).
- **Bull Gaming N.V. itself confirmed in writing to the Cyber Police of Ukraine** that the funds "actually belong to the specified online casino."
- Chainalysis analysis showed total transaction volume exceeding **$132 million** in a single analyzed period.
- The Ukrainian account holder testified he had registered the Binance account in 2018 but transferred full access to an unknown individual.
- All funds were recognized as material evidence in criminal proceedings for **fraud and money laundering**.

([Source: dev.ua](https://dev.ua/en/news/krypto-1747475639))

This seizure represents one of the largest enforcement actions against a crypto casino operator and raises serious questions about Rollbit's fund management and AML compliance.

### 3.2 Curacao License Controversy (March 2023)

In late March 2023, users noticed that Rollbit had **removed its Curacao gaming license information** from its website. The RLB token dropped **20% within 24 hours** and **37.5% over two weeks**.

Rollbit claimed the removal was due to a routine "annual renewal" process. However, this explanation was contradicted by multiple sources:

- **Curacao sub-licenses are valid indefinitely** as long as the master license remains valid (per Slotegrator's documentation), making an "annual renewal" claim dubious.
- An **anonymous casino owner told BeInCrypto** that Curacao does not disable gambling licenses during renewal periods.
- Crypto researcher **FatMan** shared conversations where Rollbit support initially dismissed the missing license as a "UI issue."

Rollbit announced in April 2023 that its license had been restored. ([Source: CoinDesk](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2023/03/31/crypto-casino-rollbits-token-drops-20-amid-licensing-concerns); [BeInCrypto](https://beincrypto.com/rollbit-allegedly-operates-crypto-casino-without-license/); [GamblingNews](https://www.gamblingnews.com/news/rollbit-assures-concerned-fans-its-license-is-not-in-trouble/))

### 3.3 Current License Status

Rollbit previously operated under a sub-license from Gaming Curacao (365/JAZ). That licensing body **ceased operations on August 18, 2024** as part of Curacao's regulatory overhaul.

Bull Gaming N.V. currently has a **pending application** (OGL/2024/1260/0494) with the new Curacao Gaming Control Board and operates under a **transitional arrangement** while the application is processed. ([Source: Curacao Gaming Control Board Certificate](https://cert.gcb.cw/))

### 3.4 Safety Ratings

Casino Guru, a major independent casino review platform, assigns Rollbit a **Low Safety Index of 4.1-4.2 out of 10**, noting that "some of the considered factors point to a low level of safety." ([Source: Casino Guru](https://casino.guru/rollbit-casino-review))

## 4. Documented Fraud Patterns

Analysis of 67 victim complaints reveals **seven distinct patterns** of predatory behavior. These are not isolated incidents — they represent systematic, repeatable tactics.

### 4.1 Win-Triggered Account Blocks (19 cases, $221,270)

The most financially damaging pattern. Accounts are locked or suspended **immediately after significant wins**, with various pretexts applied retroactively.

**How it works:**
1. User plays normally, often losing substantial sums over weeks or months
2. User wins a significant amount
3. Account is immediately locked, with one of the following justifications applied:
   - KYC verification suddenly required (despite previous deposits being accepted without it)
   - "Third-party investigation" claimed
   - Money laundering accusation without evidence
   - "Sportsbook abuse" allegation

**Notable cases:**

| Case | User | Amount | Details |
|------|------|--------|---------|
| BTC-005 | throwaccount58 | $55,000 | Won on Euro 2024 bets after $80K lifetime losses. KYC levels 1-5 completed. Muted in chat for discussing it. |
| TP-003 | Fation Larashi | $47,000 | Won $99K total. $52K withdrawn in small amounts. $47K blocked. Accused of money laundering. |
| TP-001 | Irfan | $30,000 | Lost "tens of thousands" first. Won $30K. Account suspended. Funds forfeited. |
| TP-007 | COUNTRY | $18,000 | Balance drained from $18K to $23 after becoming profitable. |

### 4.2 False Multi-Account Accusations (24 cases, $122,668)

The single most common complaint category. Users are accused of operating multiple accounts with **no evidence provided by Rollbit**.

**How it works:**
1. User completes KYC verification, sometimes through multiple escalating levels
2. User requests withdrawal
3. Rollbit claims user has "other accounts" and demands user list them
4. User denies having other accounts
5. Support goes silent or repeats the same message indefinitely

**Notable cases:**

| Case | User | Amount | Details |
|------|------|--------|---------|
| BTC-002 | cryptoshisha | $30,211 | Fully KYC verified before depositing. Accused of multi-accounting after sports wins. |
| TP-004 | chonex | $22,000 | Won $22K. Four support emails unanswered. |
| CG-001 | Anonymous | $14,800 | Deposited $3K, grew to $14.8K. Casino Guru contacted Rollbit multiple times. No response. |
| BTC-008 | steluz | $5,486 | KYC tier 3 completed with live verification. 80+ days without response from compliance. |

### 4.3 KYC Escalation Loop (9 cases, $107,110)

KYC verification is weaponized as a delay tactic with **progressive levels (1-5)** that are never truly completable.

**How it works:**
1. User requests withdrawal
2. KYC Level 1 requested (basic ID)
3. After compliance, KYC Level 2 requested (address proof)
4. After compliance, KYC Level 3 requested (selfie/video)
5. After compliance, KYC Level 4 requested (source of funds)
6. After compliance, KYC Level 5 requested (additional documentation)
7. After all levels completed, account is blocked anyway or a new justification is introduced

This pattern is documented most clearly in case BTC-005 ($55,000), where the user completed all five KYC levels including proof of income, only to be told a "third-party investigation" was in progress.

### 4.4 Selective Enforcement Against Restricted Countries (2 cases, $32,000)

Rollbit accepts deposits and allows losing play from users in restricted countries, but **only enforces geographic restrictions when users try to withdraw profits**.

**Case BTC-009 (clinexrino, $5,000):** Played from Spain for 3-4 months. Made ~100 bets. Had 5 successful withdrawals while unprofitable. When requesting a EUR 5,000 withdrawal after becoming profitable, KYC was triggered. Support member "Benji" stated: *"you are in significant profit here on Rollbit. The funds have been forfeited."*

**Case BTC-010 (Maxkej, $27,000):** Belgian user, played for 2+ years, wagered ~$125,000. Belgium was added to restricted countries after account creation with no notification. This case was eventually resolved after forum complaints.

### 4.5 Maintenance Timing Manipulation (1 case, $44,000)

**Case BTC-004 (tetaeridanus, $44,000):** User opened a 70x leverage position with $44,000 on BTC futures. Platform went offline for "scheduled maintenance" 30 minutes before US market open with only 10 minutes notice via Telegram. Before maintenance: +$2,000 profit at BTC $98,100. During maintenance: BTC dumped to $97,200. Position loss: -$36,000.

Critically, Rollbit **reimbursed users whose positions were liquidated during maintenance** but refused compensation for this user because his position remained "live" — creating a perverse incentive where the platform selectively compensated some users while denying others who suffered equal or greater losses.

### 4.6 Sportsbook Abuse Allegations (4 cases, $21,070)

Users who win consistently on sports betting are permanently banned under vague "sportsbook abuse" or "bonus abuse" terms.

**Case BTC-001 (Stakemeharder, $10,200):** Account disabled for "value-betting" at low odds (1.003-1.02) to qualify for rakeback rewards. Admin "Razer" justified the seizure stating the user "finished in profit on Rollbit" — essentially confirming that profitability itself was the trigger.

### 4.7 Support Silence (5+ cases)

After initial engagement, support stops responding entirely, leaving complaints permanently unresolved. This pattern is documented in Casino Guru mediations, where the platform's complaint resolution team contacted Rollbit multiple times and received no response.

## 5. Influencer Fraud & Market Manipulation

### 5.1 Fake Balances for Influencers

Rollbit has been documented providing **fake balances to influencers**, allowing them to showcase artificial "wins" using house money on livestreams. These staged wins are broadcast to audiences who then sign up through affiliate links, believing the demonstrated win rates are genuine. ([Source: CryptoSlate](https://cryptoslate.com/solana-based-rollbit-coin-faces-scrutiny-over-crypto-influencer-promotion-tactics/))

### 5.2 Undisclosed RLB Token Promotion Scheme

Rollbit's founder "Lucky" orchestrated a scheme offering influencers **$250,000 in RLB tokens** if the token price hit $0.20 and held for one week. The conditions required influencers to post "several organic RLB tweets" weekly — meaning the promotions were explicitly designed to appear organic and undisclosed.

Crypto researcher **Grizzly** exposed this scheme, showing that:

- Influencer **Gainzy** claimed to have purchased $400,000 in RLB, but wallet analysis revealed he was actually **selling** that amount over two weeks
- A "broad range of accounts" had been shilling RLB as an "underpriced" investment while claiming to be unaffiliated and unsponsored
- The actual arrangements were **backdoor deals** constituting potential market manipulation

([Source: CryptoSlate](https://cryptoslate.com/solana-based-rollbit-coin-faces-scrutiny-over-crypto-influencer-promotion-tactics/); [Step Finance](https://ghost.step.finance/the-controversy-surrounding-rollbit-coins-influencer-marketing-strategies/))

### 5.3 Treasury Discrepancy

Forum analysis indicated that Rollbit's platform wallets held approximately **$16 million**, while approximately **$75 million** had been transferred to Binance — raising questions about the solvency of the platform and whether user deposits were being moved off-platform.

## 6. Underage Gambling

**Case TP-038 (Joris Dajuckis):** A **16-year-old user** gambled on Rollbit for **2-3 years**, losing **$11,000** total. No age verification was implemented or enforced during this period. When the user (or their representative) requested a refund, Rollbit denied it.

While Rollbit's terms of service state users must be 18+, the platform's crypto-native onboarding process — which only requires a crypto wallet to begin playing — effectively bypasses age verification at the point of entry. KYC verification is typically only triggered when a user attempts to withdraw, not when they deposit and play, creating a system where minors can accumulate losses before any verification occurs.

## 11. Comparative Context

Rollbit's behavior patterns share characteristics with documented crypto casino exit strategies and predatory gambling operations:

1. **Asymmetric enforcement:** Deposits always accepted freely; restrictions only applied at withdrawal time
2. **Profitable user targeting:** The common thread across all complaint categories is that enforcement actions are triggered by profitability, not by actual rule violations
3. **Evidence-free accusations:** In no documented case did Rollbit provide evidence to support multi-accounting, money laundering, or abuse claims
4. **Support as delay tactic:** The progressive KYC system and non-responsive compliance department function as indefinite delay mechanisms
5. **Public pressure as sole remedy:** The only resolved cases came after sustained public forum complaints, suggesting internal processes have no genuine resolution pathway

## 12. Conclusions

The evidence compiled in this report points to a **systematic pattern of predatory behavior** by Rollbit/Bull Gaming N.V., characterized by:

1. **Fund confiscation as a business model:** The near-zero resolution rate (4.5%) and the consistent pattern of seizing funds from profitable users suggests this is not accidental but designed.

2. **Regulatory arbitrage:** Operating from Curacao with anonymous ownership, targeting users globally while claiming jurisdictional limitations when challenged.

3. **Repeat offenders:** The founders' prior involvement with CSGODiamonds — which collapsed after being caught rigging games — establishes a documented history of deceptive gambling operations.

4. **Escalating scale:** The $123 million seizure in Ukraine, combined with the acceleration of user complaints, suggests operations may be growing more aggressive over time.

5. **Influencer-driven user acquisition:** Fake balances and undisclosed promotion schemes create a pipeline of new users who are then subjected to the same confiscation patterns.
